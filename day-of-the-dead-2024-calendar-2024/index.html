The History of Day of the Dead

Day of the Dead has its roots in ancient Mexican culture, where death was viewed as a natural part of the cycle of life. The holiday was originally celebrated in the summer, but was later moved to coincide with the Catholic All Saints' Day and All Souls' Day. This blending of indigenous and Catholic traditions is what gives Day of the Dead its unique flavor.

During the holiday, families build ofrendas, or altars, to honor their deceased loved ones. These altars are decorated with flowers, candles, and photographs of the departed. Families also prepare special foods, such as pan de muerto (bread of the dead) and sugar skulls, which are colorful, decorative treats made of sugar and decorated with icing.
