The Hebrew Calendar

The Hebrew calendar is a lunar calendar used by Jewish people all over the world to determine the dates of holidays and other important events. Unlike the Gregorian calendar, which is a solar calendar, the Hebrew calendar is based on the cycles of the moon.

The Hebrew calendar has 12 months, with each month beginning with the sighting of the new moon. The months are alternately 29 and 30 days long, with an extra month added to the calendar seven times every 19 years to keep the lunar and solar calendars in sync.

According to the Hebrew calendar, the year 2024 is a leap year, which means it will have 13 months instead of the usual 12. The first month of the year is called Nisan, and it begins on the evening of March 25, 2024.

The last month of the year is called Adar II, and it begins on the evening of March 14, 2025. Therefore, the Hebrew year 5784 will begin on the evening of March 25, 2024, and end on the evening of March 14, 2025.

Why Is the Hebrew Calendar Important?

The Hebrew calendar is important to Jewish people because it determines the dates of holidays and other religious observances. Some of the most important holidays on the Hebrew calendar include:

  • Rosh Hashanah
  • Yom Kippur
  • Sukkot
  • Hanukkah
  • Purim
  • Passover
  • Shavuot

Q: Why does the Hebrew calendar have a leap year?

A: The Hebrew calendar has a leap year to keep the lunar and solar calendars in sync. Without the extra month, the holidays would gradually drift away from their original dates. By adding an extra month every seven years, the Hebrew calendar ensures that the holidays always fall at the right time of year.
